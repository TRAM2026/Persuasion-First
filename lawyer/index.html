There is a pattern most established injury firms are caught in right now.

A firm builds hundreds of five-star reviews — real clients, real verdicts, real reputation. Meanwhile, the mega-firms carpet the market: TV, billboards, six-figure Google Ads budgets bidding triple digits per click. The boutique firm competes the only way it can — by actually being better.

Then the ground shifts. Injured people stop typing into Google and start asking — ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini: "Who's the best injury lawyer near me? Should I take this settlement? Do I even have a case?"

And here's what the AI answers with: directories. Justia. Avvo. Super Lawyers. Aggregator sites listing 1,700+ attorneys, where your firm is one row in an ocean — right next to firms with a fraction of your results.

The reputation you spent fifteen years building compresses into a line item on someone else's website. That's what the AI reads. That's what it repeats.

Your marketing isn't broken. The referral source changed.

Everything your firm built still works for what it was built for. Google rankings still matter. Reviews still matter.

But AI assistants operate on a different system. They don't rank — they cite. They pull from sources they trust and repeat the names those sources repeat. Right now, for legal queries, the sources they trust most are directories — because directories structured their data for machines a decade ago and most law firms never did.

This is not a moral failing of your web guy. The discipline of being cited by AI barely existed two years ago. Almost nobody's SEO vendor is doing this work, because it isn't SEO.
