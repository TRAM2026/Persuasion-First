What's changed

The injured client stopped Googling. They started asking.

Someone who's just been hurt is scared and looking for one trustworthy answer, fast. They no longer scroll a page of ten firms — they ask: "do I have a case worth taking," "best personal injury lawyer near me," "who do I call after a wreck." The AI responds like a trusted friend: a couple of names, stated with total confidence.

Either your firm is one of those names, or, for that client, you're not in the room. This runs on a completely different system than Google — and it's new enough that almost no firm is set up for it yet.

The problem

AI is quoting the middlemen — not you.

In law, the answer layer is dominated by directories — Justia, Super Lawyers, Avvo, LawInfo — not firms. When someone asks AI for a lawyer, it cites the aggregator that lists seventeen hundred names, or the volume firm that bought its way to the top of every source. Named-partner boutiques with five hundred five-star reviews get skipped entirely, because the authority AI reads was never structured around them.

One answer. One name gets the call.

Ten blue links let a client compare. An AI answer doesn't — it hands over a short list and the first callable name usually wins the case. The mega-firms haven't locked this down yet. The answer slot only holds a few names, and the firms that claim it first are the hardest to displace, because AI reinforces the sources it already trusts.
